#### RBI Monetary Policy: Quantitative and Qualitative Tools

- Pages: 5
- Focus: Understanding the instruments used by the RBI to control money supply and direct credit flow.
- Claims: 4
- Evidence anchors:
  - Repo Rate is the rate at which RBI lends short-term to banks; increasing it decreases money supply.
  - CRR is the percentage of NDTL banks must keep as cash with RBI, earning no interest.
  - SLR is the percentage of NDTL banks must maintain in liquid assets like gold, bonds, or cash.
  - Qualitative tools include Moral Suasion, Margin Requirements (changing loan-to-value ratio), and Direct Action.

#### RBI Surplus Transfer and Economic Capital Framework (ECF)

- Pages: 5-6
- Focus: The mechanism, framework, and components involved in the transfer of RBI's surplus to the Central Government.
- Claims: 4
- Evidence anchors:
  - RBI transferred Rs 2.69 Lakh Crore surplus to the centre for 2024-25 based on the Economic Capital Framework (ECF).
  - The ECF was adopted in 2019 based on the recommendations of the Bimal Jalan Committee.
  - RBI's income sources include interest on government securities, liquidity management tools, seigniorage, and foreign currency investments.
  - Economic Capital components under ECF include Realized Equity, Contingent Risk Buffer (CRB), and Revaluation Balances.

#### India's Forex Reserves and Gold Holdings

- Pages: 6
- Focus: Composition of India's foreign exchange reserves and the rationale and risks associated with increasing gold reserves.
- Claims: 4
- Evidence anchors:
  - Gold reserves constitute nearly 15% of India's foreign reserves, reaching $102.3 billion.
  - RBI increases gold reserves for diversification (de-dollarization), risk mitigation, and hedging against inflation.
  - Risks of storing gold include reduced liquidity, zero yield, and high storage and security costs.
  - Forex reserves consist of Foreign Currency Assets (FCA), Gold, Special Drawing Rights (SDR), and Reserve Tranche Position (RTP).
